Frequently Asked Questions

Ohlone Sky is an aerial tram proposal connecting Pier 24 to Yerba Buena and Treasure Islands in San Francisco.

Equitable. Accessible. Transformative.

Ohlone Sky compliments and enhances existing local efforts by transporting patrons without using or requiring roads. Plans such as the Plan Bay Area Transit 2050+ are looking to expand and/or modify existing transit infrastructure to reduce traffic congestion. On the other hand, Ohlone Sky neither clogs nor requires roads to operate—just like aerial trams and cable-car systems in cities around the world (from Portland, Oregon to Toulouse, France to La Paz, Bolivia to Algiers, Algeria).

Further, while fiscally constrained plans such as Plan Bay Area Transit 2050+ rely on project sponsors to secure government funding, Ohlone Sky is a private-public partnership. Its projected $325-million cost comes primarily from bond financing—backed by private investors and repaid through project revenue (which comes from things such as fares and sponsorships). Ohlone Sky’s fare revenue will fund operations and maintenance, making this form of Bay Area transit self-sustaining from day one—and with no burden on taxpayers.

Ohlone Sky matters because addressing Treasure Island transit needs helps the entire Bay Area transportation ecosystem run more efficiently for all residents and visitors. Rather than altering current and future options for road traffic, this aerial tram takes to the sky with fast, seamless and delightful transit between Treasure Island, Yerba Buena Island, and San Francisco proper.

Some of Ohlone Sky’s many Bay-Area perks are:

  • geographically unconstrained and high-frequency rides (every 30 seconds)
  • high-capacity ridership potential for shuttling up to 5,500 passengers per hour, per direction
  • infrastructure that is separate from, and free of, existing traffic congestion
  • immediate accessibility from Treasure Island to job markets
  • a carbon-neutral engine for economic activity
  • a model for innovative, community-responsive infrastructure
  • a system that is self-sustaining from ridership
  • a complement to the planned bicycle/pedestrian path on the Bay Bridge’s West Span (called the Bay Skyway)
  • accessibility for all demographics and four-legged friends
  • and a private-public partnership that does not divert public dollars from other critical investments.

We are dedicated to better transit. Ohlone Sky is sustainable, efficient, timely, equitable, and accessible to all.

Aerial gondolas are ideal because they require minimal land resources, are quick to build and offer high-frequency service. They are climate-friendly, and when powered by renewables, entirely carbon-free. It complements existing transit and can accomodate induced demand with no aditional resources. Connecting Treasure Island with aerial tram is an ideal match of locale and transit modality.

Note: It does not cantilever off of the Bay Bridge. Two pylons (cable support towers) will be built into the bay between Pier 24 and Yerba Buena Island.

The Treasure Island Supplemental Transportation Study explains:

Treasure Island is a unique San Francisco island neighborhood where residents, workers, and visitors have limited transportation options to access essential services, jobs, and leisure activities on the mainland."
Currently, the only public transportation option is the Muni 25 bus, which has only one stop in Downtown San Francisco at the Transbay Terminal. Planned transportation improvements to support development including the recently launched ferry service will be phased in over time, including new ferry service to and from the San Francisco ferry terminal, new AC Transit bus service to and from downtown Oakland BART stations, expanded Muni service, an on-island shuttle, and other supplemental services like carshare and bikeshare.
While these services are planned for the future, improvements to transportation services and supplemental transportation options are needed now to better serve low-income Treasure Island residents and workers who have limited or no access to a vehicle.

Service operates daily, with cabins arriving every 30 seconds. The full ride takes twelve minutes. Board and go!

The ride is smooth, quiet, and designed for comfort. Cabins are ADA accessible, stroller- and bike-friendly, and easy to board for people of all ages and abilities. 3S gondolas are built to operate reliably in fog, rain, and harsh weather, including high winds. Operators typically suspend service only during sustained winds over 30 miles per hour—not due to safety concerns, but because cabin movement can become uncomfortable for passengers.

In the Bay Area, the Oakland Zoo Gondola (see below) travels over camels, elephants and bison. San Francisco sports a one-cabin system to Salesforce Park. In Napa, Sterling Vineyards lays claim to "the only winery in the world with an aerial gondola." In Oregon, the Portland Aerial Tram connects 10,000 daily riders from South Waterfront to Marquam Hill. In New York, the increasingly popular Roosevelt Island Tramway connects the Upper East Side to Roosevelt Island.

Meanwhile in Paris, Câble C1 is nearing completion. The busiest system worldwide is Mi Teleférico in La Paz, Bolivia, with over 300,000 passengers daily.

The Ohlone Sky aerial gondola system includes cables, stations, pylons (support towers necessary for proper tension and cable height), and gondola cabins. Cabins are suspended by cables and mechanically detach in stations to slow for boarding. As they reattach, they accelerate for the journey across the Bay.

Ohlone Sky proposes a Tricable Detachable Gondola (3S) system; the most advanced available using three cables for support and movement and stable in inclement weather.
