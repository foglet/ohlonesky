Ohlone Sky is a privately funded aerial gondola connecting San Francisco’s Pier 24 to Yerba Buena and Treasure Island.

We choose gondolas because they’re quick to build, offer high-frequency service every 20–30 seconds, and glide above congestion without the footprint of roads or rails. They’re cost-effective, climate-friendly, and when powered by renewables entirely carbon-free. It's a soaring link that complements existing systems while bridging a critical transit gap.

Treasure Island is undergoing a major revitalization, with up to 8,000 new homes planned. Ohlone Sky is designed to meet the island’s growing transit needs offering a scenic, zero-emission alternative to driving. It doesn’t replace existing systems; it complements them. By expanding mobility, we can help transform Treasure Island into a thriving, well-connected neighborhood.

Ohlone Sky is designed for scale from day one—serving an estimated two million roundtrip riders annually, including commuters, visitors, and the 20,000 future residents of Treasure Island. It offers fast, car-free access to jobs, retail, and major transit hubs like BART and Muni.

The Pier 24 station is steps from the Ferry Building, downtown and our iconic cable cars not to mention all other locales along the waterfront. This system isn’t just for tourists—it’s built for the daily needs of a growing, connected city.

The ride takes just twelve minutes end to end—faster than driving or taking a bus—embark and go. It’s smooth, quiet, and designed for comfort in all weather. 3S gondolas are engineered to operate in fog, rain, and winds up to 68 mph, keeping riders above the fray, even when the fog rolls in. Cabins are fully ADA accessible, stroller- and bike-friendly, easy to board for riders of all ages and abilities.

Breathtaking. Ohlone Sky lifts you into a rare perspective—suspended between sea and city, sky and shoreline. Below, the Bay flickers with sailboats and tides; ahead, the skyline rises in soft silhouette. You’ll see the open Pacific, five bridges, Mount Tamalpais, and Mount Diablo. Sometimes, the fog rolls in like a living tide, cloaking the city in silver, softening the edges from bay to sky.

Designed with restraint and purpose, the gondola towers are sculptural markers—framing the landscape rather than intruding on it. Each journey offers a brief stillness, a moment to see the city not as backdrop but as living terrain. This isn’t just infrastructure. It’s a quiet vantage. A moving meditation. A reminder that beauty and movement can rise together—and that transit can serve not just the present, but the future. Sustainable, generational, unforgettable.

As of spring 2025, the estimated project cost is $325 million. Capital will come primarily from bond financing, backed by private investors and repaid through project revenue—including fares and sponsorships. Operations and maintenance will be covered entirely by fare revenue, making Ohlone Sky self-sustaining from day one. There is no burden on taxpayers.

Ohlone Sky sets aside 10% of its annual revenue to nourish what moves us most: the communities we live in, the climate we share, and the cultures that shape us. As a certified B Corp, we see transit not just as a path from here to there, but as a thread that binds purpose to place. This is movement with meaning—designed to move society forward.

Ohlone Sky takes its name in recognition of that history—a gesture of respect, not replacement. We acknowledge the Ramaytush Ohlone people, who are the traditional custodians of this land. We pay our respects to the Ramaytush Ohlone elders—past, present, and future—who call this place, the lands that this project sits upon, their home.

Locally, Oakland has the super cool Oakland Zoo Gondola (above during Glowfari) with a six-county bird's-eye view over live oak trees, bison, camels and elephants. San Francisco sports a one-cabin system to Salesforce Park from Mission Street. In Napa, Sterling Vineyards lays claim to "the only winery in the world with an aerial gondola." In Oregon, the Portland Aerial Tram connects South Waterfront to Marquam Hill with 10,000 daily riders. In New York, the Roosevelt Island Tramway connects the Upper East Side to Roosevelt Island.

Meanwhile in Paris, our sister city, they are in the homestretch of their first urban cable car Câble C1. The busiest gondola system worldwide is Mi Teleférico in La Paz, Bolivia, serving more than 300,000 passengers daily. There are more than 10,000 installations worldwide.

The Ohlone Sky aerial gondola system, as with all aerial gondola systems, includes cables, passenger stations, towers, and gondola cabins. In an aerial transit system, cabins are suspended above grade by cables strung between stations and towers.

When a cabin enters a station it is mechanically detached from the main cable and slowed to 1 mile per hour as it travels into, and out of the station. At this speed people-who walk at an average speed of 3 miles per hour-can easily walk into or out of the cabin. As cabins enter the boarding area, the doors open and passengers exit, then new passengers get on board. The doors close, the cabin accelerates, and is clipped back onto the cable for its trip across the Bay.

Ohlone Sky proposes a Tricable Detachable Gondola (3S) system (the most advanced technology currently available and invented 25 years ago), utilizing high-capacity cabins to efficiently transport passengers. 3S systems use three steel cables to support and move cabins. Two stationary cables provide stabilizing support, while a third cable continuously circulates the cabins.
